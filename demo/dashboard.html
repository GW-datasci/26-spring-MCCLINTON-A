📉 Change Since Peak

Incidence peaked in 1993 following widespread PSA testing, then fell 43% by 2021. Mortality has fallen steadily — down ~52% from its peak.

Good news: Survival for localized prostate cancer is ~99%. Early detection still matters.

📅 Guideline Timeline

  • 📍 1987: PSA test introduced
  • 📍 2008: USPSTF: caution for 75+
  • 📍 2012: USPSTF: discouraged for all
  • 📍 2018: USPSTF: shared decision-making for 55–69

⚠️ The Black-White Gap

Black men face the highest burden of any group — and the disparity is not explained by biology alone.

📊 Rate Ratio: Black men are diagnosed at ~1.7× the rate of white men and die from prostate cancer at ~2.1× the rate. This gap has persisted for decades and is a central focus of public health research.

🔍 Why the Disparity?

  • Less access to routine screening
  • Later stage at diagnosis on average
  • Differences in treatment receipt
  • Higher prevalence of aggressive tumor biology
  • Socioeconomic and geographic barriers

What This Means for Men Ages 50–65

Plain-language takeaways based on population-level data. This is not medical advice — always consult your doctor.

📊
Important

How Common Is Prostate Cancer?

About 1 in 8 men will be diagnosed with prostate cancer in their lifetime. It is the most common cancer in American men (excluding skin cancer), and the second-leading cause of cancer death.

But it's also one of the most treatable cancers when caught early — 5-year survival for localized disease is approximately 99%.

🩺
Your Age Group

Should You Get Screened?

The USPSTF recommends that men aged 55 to 69 make an individual decision about PSA testing with their doctor, weighing personal risk factors, values, and preferences.

For men 70 and older, routine PSA screening is generally not recommended, as potential harms outweigh benefits.

⚠️
High Risk Groups

Who Faces the Highest Risk?

You may be at higher risk if:

  • You are Black / African American — 1.7× more likely to be diagnosed, 2.1× more likely to die from it
  • You have a family history — father or brother diagnosed, especially before age 65
  • You are over 50 — risk increases sharply with age

If any of these apply, earlier screening conversations may be warranted.

📉
Good News

The Mortality Trend Is Improving

Prostate cancer mortality has fallen by more than 50% since its peak in the early 1990s — largely due to earlier detection and improved treatments.

However, late-stage diagnoses are rising again since 2012, which may eventually reverse some of this progress if screening declines further.

🌍
Where You Live Matters

Geography and Access Affect Outcomes

Southern states have significantly higher prostate cancer mortality than Western states. These differences largely reflect disparities in healthcare access, screening availability, and the demographics of each region.

If you live in a high-burden state, proactive screening conversations are especially important.

🔬
Treatment Is Improving

Modern Treatment Options

Treatment for prostate cancer has advanced significantly. Options range from active surveillance (watchful waiting for slow-growing tumors) to surgery, radiation, hormone therapy, and newer targeted therapies.

Not all prostate cancers need immediate treatment — some are slow-growing and may never cause symptoms. A diagnosis is not automatically an emergency.

📎

About This Dashboard

This dashboard was developed as a GWU Data Science Capstone project. Data visualized here are modeled on publicly available cancer surveillance sources including the NCI SEER Program, CDC WONDER, and USPSTF guidelines. Web scraping was used to collect current ACS statistics and USPSTF policy dates. Data mining techniques including trend analysis, rate-ratio computation, and cluster profiling were applied to surface key patterns. All findings should be interpreted as population-level estimates — individual risk varies. This is not medical advice.
