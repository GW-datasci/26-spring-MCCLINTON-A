Key Finding
Incidence rates peaked in 2022, rebounding from a 2013 low of 84.2/100K. This reflects increased screening and PSA testing adoption. Meanwhile, mortality has remained relatively stable around 19-20/100K, suggesting improved treatment outcomes.

Clinical Implications
The dramatic decline in localized diagnoses (2000-2013) reflects the 2012 USPSTF recommendation against PSA screening for average-risk men. The subsequent rise (2015+) indicates renewed clinical interest and informed screening decisions among eligible populations.

Burden of Illness: Incidence vs Mortality by Race

Health Disparity
Non-Hispanic Black men experience a 60% higher incidence rate (175.2/100K) and 109% higher mortality rate (37.9/100K) compared to Non-Hispanic White men. This disparity reflects differences in screening access, treatment patterns, and potential biological factors.

Geographic Variation in Prostate Cancer Rates by US State

Regional Patterns
Southeastern and Mid-Atlantic states show higher incidence and mortality rates. Potential factors include older populations, differential screening practices, healthcare access, and socioeconomic factors. Western states generally show lower rates.

Survival and Age Distribution

5-Year Relative Survival Rate by Stage at Diagnosis

Age Distribution of Prostate Cancer Cases

Early Detection Saves Lives
Nearly 100% of men diagnosed with localized prostate cancer survive 5+ years. In contrast, those with distant (metastatic) disease at diagnosis have only 38% survival. This stark difference underscores the critical importance of early detection and screening in eligible populations.

Screening Recommendations
For average-risk men: Discuss PSA screening at age 55-69. Decision should be individualized after discussion with your healthcare provider about benefits and risks.
Risk Factors
Age (risk increases after 65), race/ethnicity (Black men have higher risk), family history (first-degree relative with prostate cancer), and obesity may increase risk.
Take Action
Talk to your healthcare provider about your individual risk. Screening decisions should balance early detection benefits against potential harms like false positives and overtreatment.
