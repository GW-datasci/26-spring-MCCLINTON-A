🚨 Key Trend Alert: After declining 6.4% per year from 2007–2014 (following USPSTF's Grade D against routine PSA screening), prostate cancer incidence reversed — rising 3.0% annually since 2014. Distant-stage diagnoses are now rising at 4.7% per year, while mortality decline has slowed from ~3.5%/yr to just 0.6%/yr. The screening pendulum has real consequences.

📋 USPSTF Screening Guidance — What Does This Mean for You?

C
Ages 55–69: Individualized Decision (Grade C)
The USPSTF recommends that men 55–69 have a conversation with their doctor about whether PSA-based screening is right for them individually, weighing early detection benefits against overdiagnosis risks.
!
Black Men & Family History: Start Earlier
If you are Black/African American, or have a first-degree relative (father or brother) diagnosed with prostate cancer before age 65, many guidelines recommend starting the screening conversation at age 40–45, not 55.
D
Ages 70+: Generally Not Recommended (Grade D)
The USPSTF gives a Grade D recommendation against routine PSA screening for men 70 and older — potential harms of overdiagnosis generally outweigh benefits. Individual circumstances vary.
💬
The Most Important Action: Talk to Your Doctor
PSA screening is not a simple yes/no decision. Your doctor can weigh your specific risk factors, family history, and overall health to help you make the right choice. The conversation itself is the intervention.

🔧 Data Collection & Methodology

Data were collected via a Python pipeline using requests and BeautifulSoup4 for web scraping (targeting ACS, NCI SEER, PMC articles, and USPSTF guidelines), plus the SEER API and CDC WONDER API for programmatic data access, and direct CSV downloads from CDC USCS public datasets. All data were standardized, merged, and loaded into a SQLite database using pandas. Nine tables cover 201+ data points spanning 2000–2022. Visualizations are built with Plotly and served via a Flask web application with a REST API backend. All figures represent age-adjusted rates per 100,000 men unless otherwise noted. Data limitations include reporting delays, suppressed small counts for certain subgroups, and demographic classification changes over time.
